Executive Summary

DOG Bakery by Pets Naturally has been hand-crafting gourmet dog treats in Traverse City, Michigan since 2006. The product is exceptional. The customers are loyal — 217 Facebook reviews with a perfect 100% recommendation rate, 105 Google reviews at 4.8 stars. The woman-owned story is compelling. The visual product is made for social media. Nearly two decades of craft have built something genuinely worth talking about.

The digital presence has not kept pace. An Instagram account with 1,070 posts and 1,611 followers. A TikTok account averaging 9,900 views per video — with only 36 followers — where the algorithm is already distributing content to thousands of people and that reach is going completely uncaptured. A Facebook page where loyal customers leave 5-star reviews and posts get buried by an algorithm that has been quietly deprioritizing the page. A website with real ingredients, a real story, and a newsletter signup — but no star ratings, no cross-selling, and a platform that hasn't been updated in years. A direct competitor running paid Google ads intercepting brand-name searches today.

The Core Finding

DOG Bakery does not have a product problem, a story problem, or a customer loyalty problem. It has a translation problem. The ingredients for a dominant digital brand are all present. What's missing is the infrastructure to translate those ingredients into reach and revenue. Every recommendation in this report addresses one specific piece of that translation — and none of it requires starting over.

1,070 posts. 1,611 followers. That works out to gaining 1.5 followers per post over the lifetime of this account. A handmade artisan food business posting at the same frequency should realistically expect 15,000–40,000 followers. The effort is real. The return on that effort is not.

What 1.19% Engagement Actually Costs

An engagement rate of 1.19% means roughly 19 people interact with each post out of 1,611 followers. The benchmark for an artisan food account at this follower range is 3–6% — meaning 48–97 interactions per post. That gap is not cosmetic. Instagram's algorithm uses engagement rate as its primary signal for whether to distribute content to people who don't already follow an account. At 1.19%, the algorithm has learned that this content doesn't generate strong responses, so it limits how widely it pushes the content. The result is a compounding cycle: low engagement leads to lower distribution, which leads to fewer new followers, which leads to lower engagement. The way out of that cycle is not posting more — it's posting content that is specifically engineered to generate real responses.

Three Specific Reasons Views Aren't Converting to Followers
  • The first 0.5 seconds of each Reel aren't doing enough work. On Instagram, a viewer decides whether to keep watching or scroll within half a second of a video starting. The accounts in this category consistently hitting 20k–100k views open with their single most compelling visual — icing being piped onto a still-warm treat, a dog's eyes going wide at a birthday cookie, the reveal of a full decorated tray. If the first frame is a static wide shot of a display case or a slow pan to establish context, a significant portion of viewers are already gone before the interesting part begins. Editing every Reel to open with its best moment — not its logical beginning — is a change that costs nothing and can meaningfully increase view duration, which is the most important signal Instagram uses to decide how widely to push a video.
  • The hashtags are too large to surface in. The Easter video uses #dogsofinstagram, #dogtreats, and #easterbunny. These tags collectively have hundreds of millions of posts. Content posted there competes with an enormous volume of other posts and gets buried almost immediately. The correct approach is 3–5 tags in the 50,000–500,000 post range, where the account can actually rank and be discovered by people actively browsing that niche: #gourmetdogtreats, #dogbakery, #handmadedogtreats, #michigandogs, #traversecity. Smaller pools, visible fish.
  • The profile isn't converting Reel viewers into followers. When someone sees a Reel and taps the profile, they encounter two things: a bio that's truncated on mobile — the location disappears behind a "more" tap — and two Highlights with identical generic paw print covers that communicate nothing about the product range. The Reel earns the profile visit. The profile doesn't close the follow. Fixing the bio and rebuilding the Highlights takes two hours total and directly impacts what percentage of every future Reel viewer converts into a follower.
The Bio — Exact Fix

Current bio truncates on mobile, hiding the location. A rewritten version that fits within Instagram's 150-character visible limit: "Hand-baked gourmet dog treats 🐾 Woman-owned · Traverse City, MI · Est. 2006 · Free shipping over $75" — followed by a direct website link. Everything visible, nothing cut off, location front and center for tourist discovery.

The Highlights — What They Should Be

Story Highlights are the first interactive element a new visitor sees on the profile — they function as a brand navigation menu. Two identical sage-green paw print Highlights communicate nothing about what DOG Bakery sells, what its range is, or what makes it worth following. A business with this product depth should have 8 Highlights with custom-branded covers made in Canva (free, approximately two hours): New Arrivals, Seasonal, Birthday, Behind the Scenes, Customer Dogs, Our Ingredients, Reviews, How to Order. Every new visitor from a Reel currently lands on a profile with two identical icons. After this change, they land on a complete, navigable brand.

Why 9,900 Views With 36 Followers Is Remarkable

On Instagram, reach is largely dictated by follower count. An account with 36 followers realistically expects 50–100 views per post. TikTok works differently — its algorithm distributes content based on how people respond to it, entirely independent of whether they follow the account. DOG Bakery is averaging 9,900 views per video not because of an existing audience, but because TikTok has tested the content on small groups of users, seen strong watch time, and pushed it further. That is algorithmic distribution that most brands pay agencies to try to manufacture. It's happening here organically.

It's worth being honest: TikTok views are not consistent. A 25,000-view video doesn't guarantee the next video performs the same way. The algorithm is responsive to each individual piece of content, not a blanket endorsement of the account. What the 9,900 average does indicate is a baseline of solid distribution — the content profile TikTok has built for this account is working. The goal is to understand why and do more of it.

The Revenue Gap — 9,900 Views With No Purchase Path

Here is the central problem: thousands of people are watching these videos per post, the caption says "Shop link in bio," and there is no TikTok Shop connected to the account. That means a viewer who wants to buy has to: remember to tap the bio, find the Linktree link, tap through to the website, find the right product, and complete a checkout on a legacy platform not optimized for mobile. Every one of those steps loses a percentage of the audience. Most of them never make it.

TikTok Shop changes this entirely. With Shop enabled, a product tag lives directly in the video. A viewer watches the Easter bunny treats, taps the tag, and purchases without leaving TikTok. That is a complete sales funnel inside a single video. With 9,900 average views and a 0.5% purchase conversion rate — a conservative estimate for an engaged, niche audience — that's 49 sales per video. At a $25 average order value, that's $1,225 in revenue per video posted. The content is already reaching people. The mechanism to turn those viewers into buyers doesn't yet exist.

What's Working and What to Build On

The POV format is clearly resonating — "POV: Your dog found the pot of gold" reached 25,000 views. This format works because it puts the viewer in the dog's perspective, creating an immediate emotional hook before any product is shown. It's a format worth using regularly across the product range: seasonal drops, birthday treats, new arrivals. Not every video needs to be a POV — variety keeps the feed engaging — but when a format has already proven it can reach 25,000 people on this specific account, it deserves to be the backbone of the content strategy.

The account also occasionally reposts short, charming dog videos from other creators — four posts visible from other accounts. This shows a good instinct for what the audience enjoys, and there's nothing wrong with it in small amounts. The one consideration worth noting is that TikTok's algorithm builds a content profile for each account to determine who to show it to. An account that's primarily original dog treat content, with occasional dog repost content, keeps that profile clear and consistent. It's a small thing, but worth keeping in mind as the account grows.

The Most Underused Asset in This Audit

Why Post Engagement Is Lower Than It Should Be

Averaging around 20 likes on a 5,500-follower Facebook page is below where this audience could be performing. A benchmark engagement rate of 1–3% on a 5,500-follower page would generate 55–165 interactions per post. Facebook's algorithm distributes content partly based on how prior posts have performed — a consistent pattern of low-engagement posts trains the algorithm to show content to fewer followers over time. This isn't a sign that followers don't care about DOG Bakery. The 100% review score proves they do. It's a sign that the type of content being posted isn't generating the kind of response that signals the algorithm to push it further.

The fastest reset is a post specifically designed to generate comments. Facebook's ranking algorithm weights comments at roughly 8–10x the value of a like. A simple direct question — "What's your dog's all-time favorite treat flavor? Peanut butter, carrot, or something else?" — generates comments. Comments increase reach. Increased reach generates more comments and reactions on the next post. This is where to start.

The Linktree Note

Many businesses use Linktree in their bios — including competitors in this space — so it's a reasonable choice. The one practical consideration: each Linktree click doesn't register as direct traffic to the website, which is a minor but real factor in how Google measures domain authority over time. It also adds one tap between "I want to buy" and "I'm on the product page," and in mobile ecommerce, every extra step reduces conversion. When the website migrates to Shopify (Action 03), replacing the Linktree with a direct site link is worth considering — Shopify's homepage can serve as the navigation hub that Linktree currently is.

The Woman-Owned Story — In the Bio, Nowhere Else

It's in the bio. It's not in the content. The most engaged customers on Facebook — the ones writing enthusiastic reviews, celebrating staff anniversaries in the comments — are already emotionally connected to the people behind this business. A content series that leads with the story: how DOG Bakery started, a day in the bakery, the team, 18 years of treats — would generate more engagement than any product post. This kind of content is also highly shareable, which is the primary mechanism for organic follower growth on Facebook in 2026. The story exists. It just hasn't been made into content.

Website — Real Strengths, Structural Gaps

There is more on the website than is immediately visible. A full About page with genuine brand story — "quality is never an accident," 20+ flavors, an onsite bakery, a staff page, a store tour. A newsletter signup. Detailed ingredient lists and guaranteed analysis on product pages. These are real assets and they reflect a business that takes its product seriously.

The gaps aren't about content that's missing — they're about infrastructure that isn't working hard enough for the business. The platform, the lack of star ratings, and the absence of cross-selling are the three things costing the most revenue right now.

The clearest indicator of how long the platform has gone without a meaningful update: product pages include social sharing buttons for Digg and StumbleUpon — platforms that shut down in 2018 and 2017 respectively. This signals to first-time visitors, however subtly, that the site hasn't been actively maintained. First impressions on product pages form in under 400 milliseconds, and a page that feels dated erodes trust before a customer reads a single word of product description.

The Star Rating Gap — Why It Matters More Than Any Other Fix

Text reviews exist on some product pages. The Testimonials section has real, enthusiastic customer quotes. The problem is that in 2026, a shopper who lands on a product page from TikTok or Instagram — someone who has never heard of DOG Bakery before — scans for a star rating within the first two seconds. If there isn't one, they make a risk assessment: this product has no verified reviews. For a $20–$40 treat box purchase from an unfamiliar brand, that assessment often ends in a closed tab. The text reviews are real and they're good. They're just invisible to the buyers who need to see them most.

When the website migrates to Shopify, installing a review app like Loox or Judge.me converts existing text reviews into a star-rated system and makes collecting new ones straightforward. Emailing the existing customer base and asking them to leave a rated review — offering 10% off the next order as a thank-you — should yield 50+ star-rated reviews on key products within two weeks of launch. That changes the conversion rate of every product page immediately and permanently.

The Cross-Sell Problem — Every Product Page Is a Dead End

A customer who finds the birthday cookie and wants to buy it for their dog's birthday lands on a product page with no suggestion that the birthday cake, birthday bone, or birthday box exist. They buy the one item they found. The order closes. The opportunity to increase that order value ends. On Shopify, "Frequently Bought Together" and "You May Also Like" sections are native features that populate automatically based on purchase behavior. The average order value increase from a well-configured cross-sell is 10–30%. On every transaction. For no additional marketing spend.

The Newsletter — One Change That Makes It Work

The newsletter signup is there — "Sign up to receive coupons, discounts and product updates." That's a reasonable offer. What would make it significantly more effective is a specific, immediate incentive: "Sign up and get 15% off your first order." The difference between "sign up for updates" and "sign up and get something right now" is the difference between a 0.5% and a 3–6% signup rate. The email list that gets built from that offer is the highest-lifetime-value asset in ecommerce — each subscriber averages $5–20 in annual revenue. A list of 2,000 subscribers built over 12 months generates $10,000–$40,000 in annual revenue that didn't exist before.
